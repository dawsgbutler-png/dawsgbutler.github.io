Sherpa Cleaning Drone Production

Ground-up assembly, wiring, testing, troubleshooting, and production improvement for Lucid Bots’ commercial exterior-cleaning drone.

CONTEXTLUCID Bots
TIMELINE2025–2026
COLLABORATIONCross-functional production team
THE CHALLENGE

Build reliable commercial cleaning drones during a high-demand production period while maintaining quality across mechanical assembly, power systems, wiring, controls, printed components, and final testing.

MY ROLE

I worked with the production team from the ground up on complete drone builds. That included arm and frame assembly, wiring, component installation, fit-up corrections, testing, troubleshooting, and communicating recurring issues back to engineers and team leads.

ENGINEERING PROCESS

How the work moved from problem to functioning result.

01

Build complete systems

Rather than focusing on one repetitive subassembly, I learned the full product and moved across arms, frame hardware, electronics, wiring, and final integration.

02

Use in-house manufacturing

The vertically integrated environment included extensive in-house 3D printing, giving me practical experience with print quality, part fit, iteration, and the relationship between CAD geometry and assembly.

03

Troubleshoot at the source

When a component did not fit or a system did not behave correctly, I traced the problem through the mechanical and electrical interfaces instead of treating it as an isolated defect.

04

Improve production flow

Our team recovered a major production backlog and, during the busy season, eventually reached a pace that temporarily outstripped sales demand and created finished inventory.

OUTCOME

The experience taught me how a real robotics product moves from components to a tested machine. I gained confidence working across mechanical and electrical systems while helping a small manufacturing team improve throughput without losing sight of quality.

WHAT I LEARNED

A well-designed product is only successful when it can be assembled consistently. The strongest engineering improvements often came from technicians noticing recurring fit, routing, access, or quality problems and communicating them clearly.
