## Portfolio projects

### 01. Assistive Navigation Robot

**Context:** University Coding Course  
**Timeline:** Six-month design cycle  
**Collaboration:** Team of five  
**Tools:** MATLAB, Sensor integration, COTS hardware, Mechanical assembly, Rapid prototyping

**One-line summary:** A six-month team project combining spatial detection, audible warnings, and separation alerts to support safer walking for a visually impaired user.

**Challenge:** Create an affordable assistive device that could help a visually impaired person recognize nearby obstacles and alert a trusted contact if the user became separated from the robot. The project required our team to integrate code, sensors, electronics, mechanical assembly, and a simple user interaction into one working prototype.

**My role:** I worked on a five-person team through a full six-month design and build cycle. My responsibilities included assembly, component integration, MATLAB development, troubleshooting, test planning, and helping translate the accessibility concept into a functioning prototype.

**Engineering process:**
- **Define the user need:** We chose an assistive walking concept rather than a general demonstration robot, which gave every design decision a clear purpose.
- **Integrate sensing and feedback:** The robot used spatial detection to identify nearby obstacles and produce an audible warning as the user approached them.
- **Add separation monitoring:** A tether or “leash” provided another safety layer. If the connection pulled away, the system initiated a message to a designated contact.
- **Test and refine:** We repeatedly adjusted code, wiring, sensor behavior, and the physical layout until the hardware and software behaved as one system.

**Outcome:** The prototype completed the required functions, and our team earned an A on the final project. More importantly, it showed me how mechanical systems, sensors, and software must be designed together around the person using them.

**What I learned:** This was my steepest coding learning curve. I did not begin the project as a confident programmer, but six months of MATLAB development and hardware troubleshooting made me much more comfortable learning unfamiliar technical tools and contributing across disciplines.

### 05. AI Trading Decision System

**Context:** Independent Project  
**Timeline:** 2026–present  
**Collaboration:** Self-directed with AI-assisted development  
**Tools:** Python, Alpaca API, Market data APIs, Automation, Backtesting, Risk controls, Dashboard design

**One-line summary:** A self-directed paper-trading platform built to test AI-assisted decision logic, risk controls, scanning, portfolio rotation, backtesting, and live monitoring.

**Challenge:** Explore how far I could push AI-assisted development by creating a system that evaluates a large stock universe, converts market data into structured decisions, manages risk, executes simulated orders, and explains what it is doing.

**My role:** I defined the system requirements, tested the logic, reviewed failures, organized modules, and iterated on the scanner, risk manager, rotation engine, execution workflow, logging, dashboard, and replay tests. The project was built in my free time to learn coding, APIs, automation, and quantitative decision systems.

**Engineering process:**
- **Design the architecture:** The system separates market data, feature generation, regime detection, scoring, risk management, portfolio rotation, execution, and logging so each part can be tested and improved.
- **Build a repeatable loop:** Every cycle checks system health, reads current data, evaluates the market regime, scores candidates, reviews positions, applies risk rules, and records the outcome.
- **Test before trusting:** Paper trading and historical replay testing expose weaknesses without risking real capital.
- **Improve transparency:** Mission Control summarizes system status, decisions, errors, blocked trades, position changes, and performance instead of hiding the process behind a single output.

**Outcome:** The project dramatically expanded my comfort with Python, APIs, debugging, data structures, automation, and system reliability. It also taught me that a sophisticated dashboard is less important than disciplined testing and clear risk controls.

**What I learned:** AI can accelerate development, but it does not replace engineering judgment. I still have to define requirements, detect incorrect assumptions, compare results, manage edge cases, and decide whether the system is behaving logically.

### 06. Before the Recordable

**Context:** Gaylor Electric Capstone  
**Timeline:** Summer 2026  
**Collaboration:** Capstone project  
**Tools:** QR reporting, Dashboard design, Process mapping, Safety analytics, Presentation

**One-line summary:** A proactive construction safety concept that turns fast QR-based near-miss reports into leading indicators, corrective actions, and visible trends.

**Challenge:** Near misses often contain the earliest evidence of a dangerous pattern, but reporting friction and disconnected follow-up can cause those signals to disappear. The goal was to make reporting easier and turn individual observations into useful leading indicators.

**My role:** I developed the concept for a QR-code reporting workflow and a management dashboard that could organize hazard categories, locations, response times, corrective actions, and recurring patterns. I then turned the idea into a structured capstone presentation focused on practical jobsite adoption.

**Engineering process:**
- **Reduce reporting friction:** A QR code gives field personnel a direct path to a short report instead of requiring a complicated process.
- **Structure the data:** Consistent fields make it possible to compare events by hazard type, location, timing, and response status.
- **Reveal weak signals:** A dashboard converts isolated observations into trends that safety leaders can act on before an incident becomes recordable.
- **Close the loop:** Response tracking and corrective-action visibility show whether a reported concern was acknowledged, assigned, and resolved.

**Outcome:** Before the Recordable reframes near-miss reporting as an operational feedback system rather than a compliance task. The concept is designed to help project teams see risk sooner, respond faster, and learn across multiple reports.

**What I learned:** A useful safety solution must respect field reality. The strongest concept is not the one with the most features—it is the one workers will actually use and leaders can act on quickly.

**Primary media:** safety-platform.webp
